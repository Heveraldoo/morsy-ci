What do these results mean?

Importance Coefficients

Importance coefficients are the weights assigned to sub-indicators before aggregation. They slightly differentiate the influence of the sub-indicator on the composite indicator. The coefficients are reported before the construction of the composite indicator, and their real influence is only known later, based on the relationship between the sub-indicator and the composite indicator [1].

Composite Indicator Quality

These parameters signal the composite indicator's quality and reliability [2].

Explanatory power: reflects the ability of the composite indicator to capture the concept of the multidimensional phenomenon from the perspective of conceptually significant variables of the composite indicator. For example, the correlation of income with a composite indicator of poverty should present a moderate or greater correlation to signal adequate informational power.

Informational power: is associated with the transfer of information from the input data to the composite indicator. It measures the proportion of the original data captured by the composite indicator. Informational power, also known as informational loss, is useful for verifying the internal consistency of the composite indicator.

Discriminating power: measures the informational diversity of the composite indicator. Entropy indices or dispersion statistics can measure it. Composite indicators with low informational power have similar scores, which makes it difficult to differentiate between decision-making units.

Uncertainty: this is the most frequently used quality parameter. It measures the variation in ranking between the reference composite indicator and composite indicators constructed using other weights, normalization, and aggregation functions, signaling the stability of the internal structure of the composite indicator.

Popular composite indicators

These methods differ essentially in how the sub-indicators are weighted [3].

Benefit of Doubt (BoD): sub-indicators weighted to maximize scores. The BoD uses a weight matrix. The weights of the sub-indicators vary among themselves and the decision-making units. This weighting logic allows spatial heterogeneity to be operationalized when the decision-making units are geographic (city, neighborhoods, etc.) [4].

Entropy: the entropy weighting method assumes that higher weights should be assigned to sub-indicators with greater informational diversity, contributing more to differentiating decision-making units [5].

Principal Component Analysis: aims to construct a composite indicator that extracts the largest proportion of the information in the original data, maximizing the variance extracted. This logic causes higher weights to be assigned to the most intercorrelated sub-indicators [6].

Equal Weights: assigns equal weights to all sub-indicators. This weighting logic prioritizes simplicity of execution and explanation of results. In addition, this weighting scheme proves useful when the multidimensional phenomenon does not have a well-established conceptual framework [7].

A complete and detailed explanation of the subject can be found in the OECD guide for constructing composite indicators [8]
